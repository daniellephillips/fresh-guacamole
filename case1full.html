Background

Intel XDK was a free, cross-platform IDE for building and publishing HTML5 apps across web and native platforms. Apps were written in HTML, JavaScript, and CSS, with a single code base. Web developers quickly adopted the product because there was no need to learn new languages or acquire special skills.

Role and Challenges

In 2016 I became the design lead for this project. The product was matureing at a rapid pace asengineerng increasingly rolled out new features while old bugs and other problems persisted. Delving into existing research informed us that new users were confused by all the controls and multiple ways to preview, "test," debug, and profile projects. Functionality lived in separate tabs, which introduced context shifts and prevented users from interacting with their code while simultaneously debugging and profiling projects. The engineering, marketing, and business teams devised a plan to overhaul the GUI and focus on refining the product's core functionality.

Key Contributions

The engineering, marketing, and business teams devised a plan to overhaul the GUI and focus on refining the product's core functionality.

  • Analyzed copious amounts of user research, telemetry data, and product analytics to establish usability baselines and inform which customers we should reach out to for feedback.
  • Assessed, consolidated, and redesigned multiple versions of the revamped product.
  • Worked across four engineering teams to understand complexity, business strategy and establish faster design delivery.
  • Drove effort to engage marketing in correlating in-product actions with user engagement and retention rates.

Outcome

Improvment in New User Retention

Analysis of engagement reporting indicated a bump in our eight-week retention rates. Churn and attrition also decreased. We added filters to our reports to align with the version, edition, and build numbers users were running to see how our change affected the churn rate of each release. We were able to determine how many days/sessions/hours of runtime a typical evaluation user took before dumping the software. Moreover, we could concluded which features were being accessed and which parts were never discovered or used by churned users.
